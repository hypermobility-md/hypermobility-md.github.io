[02:47] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** Are hypermobility disorders painful? Yes, they are. And it is an important conversation because a lot of patients get dismissed because their providers don't recognize that these conditions are painful. Their families don't recognize that these conditions are painful, or other people that are supporting them. EDS, Ehlers-Danlos syndromes, and HSD, hypermobility spectrum disorders, are amongst the most painful disorders that we actually treat in medicine. They can be extremely painful, and the pain can be a lifelong thing. Now, that doesn't mean we don't have solutions for it or ways that we can improve quality of life, but pain is extremely common in these conditions.\
[03:32] When we've done studies looking at how frequently people note pain as one of the symptoms amongst people with hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, we know that there's like 90-plus percent of people who do complain of chronic pain. So it can come from a variety of different places. It can be a lifelong thing, but this is definitely something that is a significant problem.\
[03:56] People with these conditions can have multiple different types of pain. They can have what's called nociceptive pain, they can have neuropathic pain, and they can have centrally mediated pain. And so it can make it hard to treat the pain. Also, mast cell activation syndrome can contribute to the pain because mast cells and the nerve endings lie very, very close to each other. Mast cells are one of the first responders of the immune system. So when mast cells degranulate — when they get activated and they degranulate — those chemicals can act on the nerve endings and increase pain. We know that this is one of the contributing factors also to complex regional pain syndrome, which occurs more commonly in people with EDS and related disorders. So we know that mast cell activation syndrome is also an inflammatory condition, and we know that inflammation is a common problem with pain.

[08:19] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** So one of the things that I think is not discussed often enough is the genetic factors that go into how much pain a person experiences. [08:59] We know that genetic differences can also contribute to differences in levels of inflammation. So a person could be genetically predisposed towards having more pain. [10:14] Some of the other things that make a difference are prior experiences. So if you've had pain in the past, especially poorly controlled pain, it's more likely that you're going to have pain in the future. We know that the brain and the nervous system become more sensitive to pain once there's been other pain experiences. And so it's really important, if you have an injury, to address it immediately, take good care of it, try to get good pain control, because we know that there's the saying, pain begets pain. And it really is true. Once you have one chronic pain problem, you're more likely to get another one. So we really want to get a handle on things sooner rather than later.\
[10:52] We also know that coping makes a huge difference. And I'm sure you've seen this with some of your dancers. They feel like they can cope. They have a certain level of pain, but once it exceeds their level of coping, they feel like they can't cope with it. All of a sudden their pain just becomes much, much greater. And this is true for all of us — whether we are dancers with a performance coming up or anyone else — if we are experiencing something else in our life that we feel like we can't cope with, we will experience more pain because we have less ability to cope with that pain.\
[11:42] We know that different regions of the brain will process the pain and amplify it sometimes and dampen it other times. [16:58] Another really important contributor to chronic pain is — we just talked about mood, but also sleep. And I like to think of these as occurring like points of a triangle: pain, sleep, and mood. And as we know with a triangle, there are lines that connect the points. So all of these are connected and they impact each other. So if someone has a bad night of sleep, they are going to have more pain the next day. It's virtually an automatic thing. And if someone has more pain, they are likely to have worse sleep. So this is where it can really turn into a very challenging catch-22 because they have pain, so then they get bad sleep, which makes them have more pain. The exact same thing happens with mood. If a person has depression or anxiety, that increases their pain. Well, then more pain also increases depression and anxiety, which also increases the pain. So it kind of turns into a vicious cycle. And the pain, mood, and sleep are all connected to each other. So those are another really important component to be considering.\
[18:10] And then the last one I want to mention is neuroplasticity, and that is the ability of the brain to change over time. We used to think that the brain changed a lot in childhood, but basically after childhood we thought that the brain stayed static. And now we know that that's simply not the case. We know that the brain changes quite significantly. Every thought we have, every action we take strengthens the connections in our brain. So if we focus on the pain and keep thinking about the pain, that's going to strengthen those connections. If instead we are able to start working on solutions and start looking towards things that will help lessen our pain, that will help reinforce positivity and having that resource — being able to address inflammation, for example. That's a really common approach that I take in treating my patients: looking at inflammation and how much it could be contributing to someone's pain. I like to really approach things from that direction so that we're not continuing to focus so much on the pain, so that we're trying to make these other positive pathways — we're trying to reinforce those.

[19:22] **Jennifer Milner:** That makes a lot of sense. So trying to reinforce the positive pathways rather than the negative pathways, just sort of shifting where our gaze is focused. That makes sense. So what are sort of the most common myths or misunderstandings about chronic pain?

[19:49] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** I would say the most common one is that chronic pain is just acute pain but longer. We know that chronic pain, depending on the source that you look at — it used to be considered pain that was present for 6 months or longer, and now a lot of the definitions have changed to more like 3 months. But regardless of which definition you use, we know that the brain actually undergoes changes with chronic pain. What ends up happening is people think, why hasn't anything fixed my pain yet? Because they're looking for one thing to fix their pain. And maybe early on, if the pain had been properly diagnosed and the source addressed right away and treated, then the pain would have been completely resolved — maybe with one more simple strategy. But instead, as the pain is present for longer and longer, it kind of becomes like layers of an onion. Anyone who has peeled layers of an onion knows that you peel off one layer, then you've got the next layer, then you've got the next layer. When you're trying to treat chronic pain, it's kind of like peeling off those layers of the onion. It's not one thing usually that you can do to make the pain better. You need to keep peeling them off and peeling them off until you get to the root of the problem. Because the problem has been going on for so long and the changes have taken place in the brain, it's no longer a simple problem in the tissue.\
[21:20] And I think that's where people with EDS and HSD have so much difficulty, because they develop problems in so many different parts of their body and in so many different areas of tissue. So they're getting constant bombardment into the nervous system with pain generators. So it can be very challenging to keep that calmed down and to keep the nervous system from developing inflammation — and that's called neurogenic inflammation. So that's one of the most common myths.\
[21:53] Another one is that pain equals damage. So in the early phases and with acute pain, that's usually the case, although as we talked about earlier with genetics, we know that isn't always exactly the way it works. Think, for example, of Drew Brees — he developed some terrible injuries, had all these rib fractures, yet continued to play football. He had all this damage, and obviously he had pain, but yet he didn't have outrageous pain. I used to anesthetize people. They'd come in with literally digits falling off — like a finger cut off and it's hanging by a thread. Are you in any pain? No, none. So even with acute pain, those genetic differences are huge, really, really huge. But with chronic pain, it's even more significant. Pain does not equal damage. There are many, many more factors that go into how much pain we feel.

[22:55] **Jennifer Milner:** That's really interesting. That also bears repeating what you said earlier about how once it gets to being chronic pain, the thing that's causing the pain isn't the thing that caused the pain originally. Acknowledging that it has shifted to something else and has moved to perhaps a multi-systemic issue — there may have originally been one issue, or there may have originally been a trigger, but by the time it's considered chronic pain, we're not looking to fix just that ankle or deal with that back or whatever it might be and say it'll be fine. Is that correct to say?

[23:40] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** Yes, absolutely. And I think this is where people with EDS and HSD have so much difficulty. Because oftentimes the proper diagnosis is not found in the beginning because people are looking for the more common things. So if you go in with ankle pain and they do just plain X-rays, a common misconception is that if the X-ray is normal, then you're fine — you don't have a problem. Well, that only looks at the bones. That doesn't look at the soft tissue — well, it gives you a little bit of information about soft tissue, but not everything. Even an MRI doesn't tell you about everything. And these studies are static and they're done when you're lying down. So it doesn't tell you what's happening when you're moving. It doesn't tell you what's happening when you're upright.\
[24:28] So oftentimes people with EDS and HSD are not properly diagnosed when the problem first starts or the pain first starts. And so it isn't until later, when all these layers have built up, that maybe they are getting some more treatment options for their pain, but at the same time, now there are all these confounding variables. So it's harder to actually break those down. And I think a lot of patients want that one thing that's going to fix their pain. While that's totally understandable, it's just usually not realistic. [25:24] **Jennifer Milner:** A cumulative effect. So then what is your approach to the person with chronic pain?

[25:29] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** The comprehensive care plan is what I am prescribing. So a couple of things. First, we need to identify what is the source or sources of that person's pain. It depends on where they're having pain. I have literally seen 10-year-olds that have pain everywhere. Well, in that case, we really need to address what's going on in the nervous system. If they have one area of pain, then we're going to really zone in on what is going on at the tissue level, what is going on in that joint. Is there subluxation? Is there dislocation? Is there some joint instability and joint malalignment going on there? Is there a tendinopathy that has not been adequately addressed? And so the first thing is we want to identify what the source of the pain is. We may or may not do some additional imaging studies and some additional assessments — I may send them to a physical therapist to get a more detailed movement assessment, or to someone like yourself, a Pilates instructor who is super, super knowledgeable in working with people with hypermobility, who can really get a good assessment of how they're moving and really look for those dysfunctional movement patterns and try to work on those.\
[26:52] Then another part of the diagnostic workup is I also usually obtain additional lab work. Part of the challenge with people who have EDS and HSD is that their labs are usually normal, and this is very frustrating for people. And I think a lot of doctors don't understand that it's not that people want there to be something wrong with them, but they want an answer. They want to know why they're suffering. That's one thing that's really, really misunderstood. So sometimes if we dive deeper and we look at hormones and we look at nutrient levels and we look at more detailed inflammatory markers, we can get some additional information, because the routine lab work that we check most often is often normal.\
[27:45] So that's the next part that I do. And then I also really work with people on goals and realistic expectations. We want to alleviate suffering and improve quality of life, but I try to inform them that I'm not going to be able to take away your pain 100%, as much as I would love to. And in some cases, we can do that. A lot of the time it's a matter of improving quality of life and improving functional capacity, but there may still be some residual pain. But instead of having pain brain — like your brain is on fire with pain — instead you can enjoy things. You can enjoy your activities. Enjoy being with your family and things that you used to do before you had the pain problem.\
[28:30] So I kind of start with that, and then I use an acronym called MENDS-PMM as a way of myself remembering and helping other people to remember the approach that I take. [29:30] The second thing is education. They're more likely to have problems with things like PTSD and chronic pain. But there are certain treatments that we can do for things like PTSD, and if we don't address those pieces of the puzzle, we're never going to get on top of the pain. So that's extremely important.\
[37:32] The next letter is M, which stands for modalities. So modalities could include things like acupuncture or acupressure. It could include things like a TENS unit. And these can be extremely beneficial, again, when a person factors into consideration that this is one thing that we're looking for. Does it provide 10% improvement, 20%, 5%? It's all relative to what is the cost, what are the side effects, what are the risks. If it's low cost and low side effects, then it's worth adding in even if it provides a lower percentage of improvement. If it's higher cost or higher side effects, then we want it to be more beneficial in order to keep it in the comprehensive care program.\
And then the last M stands for medications. So there are a lot of different medications that people will think of when they think of treatments for chronic pain, some of which are things like antidepressants. And sometimes they can be helpful, but again, they usually don't solve the person's pain completely. It depends on what all is going on with the person. Are they experiencing depression or not? Are they experiencing anxiety or not? And these medications can be very difficult to stop. Antidepressants — one of the bigger problems with them to me is the withdrawal-type symptoms that people get when they try to stop. And sometimes their doctor doesn't give them enough guidance on how to stop them, so that can be really challenging.\
[39:15] One of my favorite medications is low-dose naltrexone, and that's an anti-opioid. And it's anti-inflammatory. It actually also benefits the immune system, and it's also very beneficial for neurogenic inflammation. It acts on glial cells and is what's called a glial cell modulator, to help reduce inflammation in the central nervous system. And it can have very, very beneficial effects. Some people don't experience quite as dramatic effects in their pain level, but they still might experience the beneficial effects on the immune system, which right now are of course super important. And this has to be compounded, so it has to come from a special type of pharmacy that can make its own medications. This is where I would really love to see some real change where these medications be covered by insurance companies, because right now a lot of insurance companies don't cover compounded medications.\
[40:21] Other medications that we would use are to control mast cell activation syndrome. Mast cell activation syndrome can contribute to headaches, it can contribute to abdominal pain, it can contribute to joint pain. And so if we get mast cell activation under better control, those types of pain can be improved.\
[40:42] The last letter, the S, stands for supplements. Of course we want to get as many of our nutrients from our diet as we possibly can, but for some of our nutrients it's really just not possible to get adequate amounts from our diet. For example, vitamin D. Depending on where we live — I live in Wisconsin, so it's a pretty northern part of the hemisphere, and in the winter it's virtually impossible to make enough vitamin D. Even in the summer, it's pretty hard. So virtually all of my patients are on a vitamin D supplement. Fortunately for vitamin D, we can monitor blood levels, which is very beneficial. For some of the other things that I would often recommend, we're not able to monitor blood levels, but we still know that they provide beneficial effects.\
[41:29] A couple of the other ones that I commonly prescribe: magnesium is one, because it can be very, very difficult to get adequate levels of magnesium from the diet. We know that a lot of people with EDS and HSD have inadequate levels of magnesium, and that can contribute to headaches, muscle spasm and muscle pain, poor sleep, mood problems, and chronic pain in general. So magnesium is a super important thing to consider. Vitamin C is another one. That's super important. Most animals can make more vitamin C — they can increase their vitamin C production when they're under stress — but humans cannot. So people who have EDS and HSD, it's like their bodies are kind of under constant stress. Having more vitamin C can be very helpful. And also vitamin C is a precursor to building connective tissue, so we want to make sure that we have definitely sufficient levels of vitamin C in order to build stronger connective tissue.
