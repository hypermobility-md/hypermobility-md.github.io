  Fatigue is common in people with chronic disease. Pain, fatigue, and
  depression can feed into each other and become a vicious cycle that’s
  difficult to break. Combating fatigue can be particularly difficult for those
  with bendy bodies and comorbidities.

  Alan Pocinki, MD, specialist in hypermobility and related autonomic and sleep
  disorders, speaks with Bendy Bodies on this complicated subject.

  Dr. Pocinki shares his “eureka” moment in linking the chronic fatigue syndrome

  different from sleepiness, and describes the underlying causes of fatigue in
  hypermobility spectrum disorders, outlining the way an overactive sympathetic
  nervous system can mimic a panic attack.

  effect of fatigue, and the role of sleep continuity. He discusses the concept
  of budgeting your energy and explores the role of anxiety in hypermobility
  spectrum disorders. Dr. Pocinki describes his approach to treating patients
  with fatigue, reveals the role hormones may play in fatigue, and shares his
  hopes for future research in fatigue and hypermobility.

[04:35] So today we want to focus on hypermobility and fatigue. If we could start with just some definitions — what is fatigue? When we're talking about fatigue, what are we talking about?

[04:45] **Alan Pocinki, MD:** Well, I think at a very basic level, fatigue is the inability or difficulty accomplishing simple tasks. [05:28] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** Okay, that's really helpful. And what sort of links do you see between hypermobility disorders and fatigue?

[05:37] **Alan Pocinki, MD:** The initial link was recognizing that a lot of patients with chronic fatigue as the primary symptom were hypermobile. [07:15] **Alan Pocinki, MD:** Well, depression is the other piece. Again, in our specialty-focused medicine in this country, patients may see a psychiatrist about their depression, but the psychiatrist doesn't know anything about managing chronic pain and probably doesn't know anything about how to fix the sleep. [07:57] I do often reassure people that depression in this context is part and parcel of their illness, and it's not any implication that they have a personality disorder or that they're not coping well. [08:28] Beyond that vicious cycle and that big three contributing to fatigue, there are certainly a host of metabolic factors that can contribute. [08:51] In the big picture, in hypermobile people, simple tasks are tiring. [09:44] And then the other piece, somewhat ironically, is autonomic dysfunction itself, which tends to be both a cause and an effect of fatigue. The body's inability to maintain an even keel, exaggerated stress responses, then overcorrections and recorrections — what I call the autonomic roller coaster — wastes a lot of energy. If you're lightheaded every time you stand up, your body kicks in too much adrenaline to correct for that. Those simple things are tiring.\
[10:15] And then, in a sort of unfortunate paradox, the more rundown and overtired you get, the worse your autonomic dysfunction tends to be. And indirectly, I think autonomic dysfunction is a major player in poor sleep quality. So it's also a big factor in fatigue.

[10:51] **Alan Pocinki, MD:** It's a huge factor. Sleep and pain are the two major things that perpetuate fatigue.
