[03:44] **Dwight McKee, M.D:** We're still learning new things about SARS-CoV-2, which is the name of the virus that causes the disease we call COVID-19. We had highly pathogenic human-to-human coronavirus epidemics with SARS — severe acute respiratory syndrome — in the early 2000s, and then MERS in the Middle East. SARS originated in China and was believed to come from a bat, as is SARS-CoV-2. MERS had its reservoir in a camel. Because these are closely related viruses, a lot of people have been going back and looking at the research that was done with the original coronaviruses.\
[04:46] This one is more infectious — substantially more infectious — and not all the reasons for that are clear. There's no immunity in humans to this virus. Coronaviruses are part of the human virome that we are exposed to all the time, and many people get coronaviruses that are passed off as the flu or are very much like the common cold. SARS was the first one that was deadly, and in fact it was more deadly than this one, but much less infectious. So there was not as much global spread of the original SARS or MERS coronaviruses.\
[05:52] One thing that was recently discovered — I heard this on an interview that David Perlmutter was doing with a virologist in New York City — is that there are prion-like sequences in what's called the spike protein. And those are called spike proteins.

**Dr. Linda Bluestein:** Okay.

[06:27] **Dwight McKee, M.D:** Those spike proteins are what bind to receptors on human cells to gain entry to the cell. The receptor that SARS used is the same one that SARS-CoV-2 uses, which is the ACE2 receptor — angiotensin-converting enzyme 2. This is an enzyme involved in blood pressure regulation and many other aspects of physiology. There are a lot of them on the lung cells called pneumocytes. They're also on macrophages, on mast cells, and on endothelial cells. So the virus can gain entry into the lung cells, into the intestinal tract cells — those are also loaded with ACE2 receptors — and into the bloodstream.\
[07:43] The most prominent organ of infection is the lungs. That seems to be the weakest link for people. This was the case with SARS as well, where it got the name severe acute respiratory syndrome: if the immune system overreacts in the lung and you get too much inflammation, you get leaking of fluids from the vascular system into the alveoli, the air sacs in the lungs, and people essentially drown.\
[08:23] So the thing that's clearly different about SARS-CoV-2 is that it is far more infectious than SARS-CoV-1. And it appears not to be the virus itself that causes the morbidity and mortality, but a dysfunctional or exaggerated immune response to the virus — the so-called cytokine storm.

[08:58] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** Interesting. So are people who have more autoimmune conditions more susceptible to cytokine storm or complications? Who is most susceptible to getting these complications from the virus?
