[37:56] So I also figured out that I needed to find ways to move more, and slowly but surely, I started to move more. As I was doing my research for that article, I read about kinesiophobia, which is fear of movement, and a lot of us with EDS and HSD develop kinesiophobia. And it makes sense. Like many of you, I had injured myself doing silly things — getting up off the sofa, picking something up, doing very, very small movements. And so then you get afraid to move because you're afraid you're going to hurt yourself. But at the same time, it's crucial for us to move because if we don't move, our muscles get weaker and weaker, and we may develop more and more dysfunctional movement patterns.\
[38:50] I actually had one of my sicker patients the other day — who is also a healthcare professional — tell me, "I know if I don't move, I will die." And she's right. Moving is absolutely essential for life. Now, there are a lot of reasons why people are not able to move a lot, and of course, those need to be addressed. Is it that they have dysfunction of their autonomic nervous system, so any kind of upright posture is extremely uncomfortable for them? Maybe their heart is racing, their breathing gets accelerated, they're dizzy, they're lightheaded, and therefore they really can't exercise in that position at all. If someone has ME/CFS, of course, they might have post-exertional malaise, and that makes movement very, very difficult. But that's where prescriptions simply to exercise without specifics can be very harmful, or at the very least, not helpful.\
[39:59] So those are a couple of things that I did differently. I also realized that learning about pain neuroscience helped me a lot. I was on call one night and I was in so much pain. This was during that period of time that I was doing locum tenens, so somewhere around 2015 or so. I went to the call room and was laying down on the bed and got at my computer and stumbled across a lecture by Dr. Dan Clauw — spelled C-L-A-U-W — and his lecture was on fibromyalgia and central sensitization. And as I listened to this lecture, I realized, "Oh my gosh, this is what has happened to my body. My body has become sensitized," because I had become sensitive to sounds, to light, to touch, to everything. And I realized this is part of why I have so much pain.\
[40:55] And through the course of that lecture, I really came to this big realization that with chronic pain, pain and damage are not equal — that you can have a lot of pain with very little damage, and actually the opposite can happen too: you can have damage in your body and not have a lot of pain. So that helped me tremendously. It allowed me to realize that what happened between my ears could help influence how much pain I felt, and influence my quality of life.\
[41:31] So I started to try to have less panic, which of course is easier said than done, but I tried to really work on my anxiety, and I also tried to have a more objective attitude. I tried to think about my symptoms in a more objective way, and I tried to report my symptoms to myself as if I was a reporter — very objectively — and try to think more concretely and try not to obsess too much over my symptoms and what was happening in my body. [42:44] I already mentioned the movement piece — that's really important. We talked a bit about education. [43:25] Before I started really working on this approach, back in 2009 for sure — 2010, '11, '12 — up until about 2015, I kept looking for one thing to take away my pain. [44:27] So it's important to keep that in mind with these modalities. [45:00] For medications, there are a variety of medications that are used off-label for EDS. Every medication is actually off-label for EDS because there are no on-label medications for EDS.
