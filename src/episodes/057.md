  Dance and other performing arts are, by nature, stressful. Stress can easily
  develop into anxiety, which is exponentially more common in the hypermobile
  population than the general population.

  How can you distinguish between stress and anxiety? What can you do to manage
  your anxiety? When is it time to seek outside help?

  We asked these questions and more of Jo-Anne La Flèche, a clinical and dance
  psychologist with a Masters in Somatics.

  Jo-Anne defines the difference between stress and anxiety, and discusses
  factors that may contribute to anxiety, both within a person and in their
  external circumstances. She breaks down how a dance environment might foster
  anxiety and shares ways to self-manage that anxiety.

  Self-care practices are offered as ways to lower anxiety, as well as seeing
  the value in recognizing things you can’t control. Jo-Anne lists signs that
  may indicate a dancer should seek help in managing their mental health and
  suggests ways to find the right therapist for you.

  Finally, Jo-Anne shares her thoughts on why it’s important to have an identity
  outside of dance.

  For all artists or even anyone struggling with anxiety, this episode is not to
  be missed.

[04:16] We want to talk today about anxiety, and I know that sometimes anxiety and stress can often get confused. So let's start by defining what is anxiety, what is stress, and what's the difference between them?

[04:31] **Jo-Anne La Flèche:** That's a very good question. Stress is an instinctive body response to a perceived threat to your well-being. I say perceived because sometimes we can feel threatened by situations that actually are not really dangerous, but for us they are. And usually that accompanies a situation where we feel challenged in some way. So stress responses are global — they're in our body all over the place, in our sympathetic system, which means that everything fires off. We'll get tense, our heart will start pounding, our head starts buzzing. We've got all these bodily responses which actually are very adaptive because that gives us extra energy to face the polar bear that we think is there and that we need to fight. So that is a really very adaptive response that we've had for millions of years. And then usually, once we have satisfied the need or the situation has been resolved, that stress signal will come down and we'll go back to our normal bodily response.\
[05:53] However, sometimes we can be going through a lot of stress factors — and especially dancers, because God knows how dance is a challenging passion and you go through all kinds of difficult situations. When those stress factors add up, we can face different mental health issues. So one of the mental health issues that we can face is anxiety.\
[06:20] Anxiety, contrary to the instinctive and global body response, is a specific reaction that we have both mentally and emotionally to an anticipated future. We are fearful of something that has not yet happened, that may not yet exist, or may not even be likely to exist. But in our own minds, we have the impression that we are facing a problem. Anxiety disorders are very common right now, especially with the COVID years and all the challenges — financially, wars, pollution, you name it. Anxiety has become one of the major mental health issues in the Western world. And anxiety can be on quite a spectrum of intensity.\
[07:28] But imagine when you couple that with the dance world, which is objectively stressful. That means that most dancers at one point in time will experience anxiety as a very normal emotion to have, whether it be before an audition, before a dance exam, or a show that's particularly important for you. So does that give you a little bit of a difference? Makes it more clear?

[08:42] **Jo-Anne La Flèche:** Well, first of all, you've got to understand how anxiety works. [09:28] So I would say that first of all, as a dancer, I've got to realize that there are a lot of physical and mental factors that can trigger anxiety. Physically, there are a lot of chemical changes going on inside the brain. If you're starting to feel hyper about something, and you're being worried about something, that may trigger an awful lot of hormones and stress hormones and physical responses. You may either want to fight something, or you may want to flee something, or the worst situation you can be in is to freeze. You're on stage and you're just invaded with anxiety — you may just freeze there and not know what to do all of a sudden, even if you know exactly all the routine and everything.\
[10:36] You may also have family predispositions. Maybe you come from an anxious family, because anxiety as a trait is hereditary. So that can be something to look at and to start reflecting on, especially as far as prevention is concerned, because that means that you are more likely to get hyped up about something than other people. That may trigger different perceptions on things — you may blow things out of proportion, or get really worried or anxious about something. Also, you can be anxious because of medication that you're taking. If you're not taking the right sort of medication, or you've taken drugs, or alcohol, or excess caffeine, or excess Gatorade, those are all things that can also trigger anxiety symptoms.\
[11:49] And psychologically, there are other things going on also. Your family comes from a certain background of values and experiences. Nothing's ever perfect in families and nothing's ever perfect in social life either. Maybe in your family situations you've learned to deal with stressful situations, but maybe in other families you haven't. And unfortunately, in certain families, dancers have gone through adverse childhood experiences that can also add an awful lot of anxiety through everyday living, never mind what's going on in the dance world.\
[12:39] So all those things can be happening at the same time. The first thing that you've got to ask yourself, when you're feeling anxious — when you're being very worried about something, you're dramatizing something, you're pulling things out of proportion, or maybe you've got this all-or-nothing kind of vision — is: what's going on? I've made one mistake and I'm sure that my life as a dancer is finished, or I'm over-focusing on how people are reacting to me. All those things can usually make you realize that you're getting a bit too worried about something and you've got to work on it.\
[13:27] So by staying in tune with your bodily sensations, if you're feeling tense and having difficulty breathing, you've got to start saying to yourself, okay, what's going on inside? What are my feelings? What are my bodily states? How am I reacting to something? Sometimes we have behaviors that actually reinforce our anxiety. For example, I'm sure that my best friend is angry at me — she hasn't said anything, but I'm sure about that. And then by being in that situation of withdrawing, things are going to get worse in my mind. So we've got to really reflect on what's happening inside and how am I reacting to that, which may help or maybe hinder my wellbeing.

[18:02] So that striving for perfection and the fact that we are harsh with ourselves is certainly part of that culture. [23:04] Is there any time that anxiety can actually be helpful? Is there such a thing as good anxiety, or times when having some worry or uncertainty can give you a helpful edge?

[23:15] **Jo-Anne La Flèche:** That's a great question, Linda, because yes, definitely. When we have a certain amount of stress or arousal, and that generates anxiety — I start worrying about what's going to happen — that can really give me an added edge about a situation that is challenging for me and that I want to be at par with. We call that performance anxiety, which is a social anxiety where I fear not being quite at par with the artistic expectations or with social expectations.\
[24:01] So when I'm preparing for a show or for a dance exam, I will probably be more motivated if I have that performance anxiety. I will be more motivated to be better prepared — I may review my work a little bit more than maybe what I would have done if I feel really cool and confident about something. [24:57] Another way I may be reacting positively to that stress is by saying to myself, I've got to really take care of myself. That will make me try and be the best that I can at that time.\
[25:23] And when I get on stage or into the dance studio for an audition, that performance anxiety — because I'm feeling aroused to a certain degree — will make me physically more conscious about everything going on. When I'm a little bit stressed out, everything lights up. My brain becomes like a light bulb that really lights up. So I'll have added concentration, added energy. I'll feel in my muscles that I'm ready to go. I'll have all that extra energy and be very aware about what's going on around me. That will certainly be a challenge and an added ace in my card game.\
[26:26] Typically, when I have performance anxiety, that will lower during the performance and after. It won't stay there. So as soon as I dance, for example, if I'm in the wings and I hear the music coming on, and I've got all these butterflies in my stomach and I feel like urinating and say, oh my God, what am I doing here? [26:51] But if I'm completely overwhelmed by my anxiety, worrying and saying negative things to myself, telling myself I'm not as good as anybody else, I'm sure I'm going to fall off pointe or fall on my face — then I can be overwhelmed, and that's when I'll start freezing. So there's a certain degree. Usually it's like an inverted U level of arousal for performance levels.

[27:35] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** That makes perfectly good sense. So for the dancers who are at the higher end of the U and maybe really having more challenges with anxiety, are there some ways that they can lower those levels of anxiety on their own?

[27:52] **Jo-Anne La Flèche:** There's a lot of self-care practices that can help. The first, most basic thing is breathing. Typically, if I'm fearful about something, the first reflex that's going to happen — if I feel that there's a polar bear coming at me and I'm their next lunch — I'm going to stop breathing. And once I do that, I'm even more tense. So the thing I've got to do is maybe train, maybe daily, to breathe deeply and calmly. Breathe in for 5 to 6 seconds with your nose, breathe out with your nose, maybe 5 to 6 seconds also, really getting to calm myself so that my nervous system starts calming down. Then I can feel myself more grounded and more at ease, and the tension is going to let go. And especially work on letting go of your body weight while you're exhaling — that's so, so important. Visualizing that you're like a big rock that's just sinking into sand.\
You can also use all kinds of imaging, which is another thing you can do — produce soothing, calming images that can help you. You can imagine a safe space while you're breathing: beside the sea, on the top of a mountain, in the middle of the woods, in your house, wherever.\
[29:51] Now, breathing and meditation and relaxation is something that needs training, just like dance. If you try and just breathe the day before a dance show or a dance event, that won't necessarily be very effective. That's the same as if you start training for an audition the day before. So you've got to do that on a daily basis. I generally advise people to do that before they go to bed. That helps them to calm down and to initiate a more restful night by just calming down, getting your system down a little bit. And you can use that as a pre-performance routine also. Very often dancers, in their pre-performance routine, are focusing an awful lot on getting the muscles all warm. But the first thing to do is to start breathing and calming down. That's very, very important.\
[31:08] I would say positive self-talk also, because when I'm anxious, I'm doing negative self-talk. I'm saying, oh, the audience won't like this — I'm catastrophizing and anticipating on things that will probably not happen. So I've got to switch that around into positive sentences such as, I'll do my best, I'm capable of doing this.\
You can also use imaging as far as preparing yourself for dance, like in mental rehearsal. You see yourself dancing in a positive and competent way. You can sense it in your body while you're going through it step by step. That'll make you feel more secure, and it'll add focus and attention to what you're doing. If you do that several weeks before and keep on doing it — and then again just before you go on stage or into the dance studio — that's likely to help you.

[32:22] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** Yes. And I know we talk a lot about imagery as a training tool. [32:57] But you also referenced something earlier about the fishbowl and separating the fish from the water. Is there value in looking at the situation and sort of examining what is the worst-case scenario, bringing a reality check to yourself? Is there value with anxiety in examining the water in some way?

[33:20] **Jo-Anne La Flèche:** Well, certainly, in those circumstances we'll be talking about mindfulness. When I start by breathing and connecting to the here and now — because anxiety is related to the future, depression is related to the past, it's a simple way of putting things — the present moment is the only place to be.\
[33:50] A certain amount of anticipation is an adaptive response. It's useful to know that I've got something to do tomorrow because that way I'm going to get ready for it. But when I'm more in the future than in the present moment, I'm generating anxious feelings because I have no control on what's going to come up tomorrow. I have absolutely no control. So I'm trying to have control on things that I can't control.\
[34:12] So if I'm in mindful practice — taking time to meditate, taking time to breathe — by doing that, I will also take a stance of being a witness instead of being a judge to my experience. Now, dancers are very used to judging themselves. Oh, I did this, I did that. And judging other people — they'll say, oh, they're a lot better than I am. They compare themselves constantly, which generates an awful lot of anxiety and negativity. But instead of being in that judge position, I try and be in the witness position. I'm just calming myself down and letting my thoughts go by as if they are clouds. I reflect on my thoughts, but not as a reality — saying to myself, those are my thoughts, but I am not my thoughts, and my thoughts are not necessarily reality. They come from me, but I can change them if they're not helping me.\
[35:37] That will help me to see: okay, what's happening in the fishbowl? What kind of water? What's happened this week? And that's going to start helping me to find what are the triggers of my anxiety. Why am I more anxious this week?\
[35:58] As you said, dancers will have a certain baseline of anxiety you just can't eliminate because it goes with the territory, especially if you're hypermobile. But after that, what's the added stuff? So if I'm breathing and I'm in this mindful set of thinking — not judging anything, just reflecting on what's happening — I'll be able to say to myself: okay, my teacher said this, my mother told me such-and-such, my partner said something, I'm disappointed about myself, whatever. [37:12] That can help me develop what we call self-compassion — being my own best friend. What would I say to my best friend if they came to me with certain things? [39:09] But are there signs that a dancer may want to seek help in dealing with their anxiety and not try to handle it all themselves?

[39:17] **Jo-Anne La Flèche:** Yes. I think that if you feel anxious most of the time, and that's been lasting — I would say 6 months — that can be an interesting criteria. [39:52] Remember that anxiety is on a spectrum of intensity. On the lighter end are adjustment disorders. [45:13] Which is very different from having a beer or a glass of wine on Saturday night. That's fine.
