I have maybe a vision or an idea, but I'm open — I'm in collaboration with the dancers. So I think that fact alone should make it healthier on the body.

[34:52] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** That sounds amazing and fabulous, and I think that's a great way to think about it — that if a dancer is feeling like their body needs a little more rest at that time, like you said, being mentally 110% there but maybe marking some things, that makes a lot of sense. In reality, do you see that happening very often? Have we come a long way in that regard?

[35:27] **Adji Cissoko:** I really think it depends on the choreographer. Some choreographers, they don't want to hear that. They're so focused on what they're envisioning that it's almost like they have no time for injuries. So I'm sure that still happens a lot and that the dancer will just not speak up. And I think that's also a problem, because we want to create a space where the dancer does feel like they can speak up. If they don't say anything, the choreographer doesn't know, and then they keep pushing and keep demanding. And it creates that circle where injury is bound to happen.\
So it's about creating that safe space, but also knowing we are having to create something. So be smart about it. And also, after rehearsal, it is the dancer's responsibility to take care of their bodies. You shouldn't go clubbing afterwards if your knee is already hurting. Get that sleep, get the best nutrition, take care of your body so that you can give 100% in rehearsal.\
[36:32] But also the rehearsal director's or choreographer's responsibility is to create a space where the dancer does feel safe to speak up — which I'm hoping is becoming more and more common. I feel like just a few weeks ago when I was creating with Nashville Ballet, there were people saying, oh, my back is hurting. And I told them, I don't need you to do this lift every day. I have seen it. I trust that it's okay. Save that for when we do the run-throughs. Especially when you're repeating things for music or formations, or maybe even while I'm still figuring it out, I'm like, I don't need to see it full out. I don't need to see the lift. I just need you to be in the right space at the right time and do the musicality so that I can fix what I need to fix. So I think it's also really good to communicate that to the dancers so that they know, oh, this isn't about me dancing right now. This is about something else.

[37:27] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** And I would love for every choreographer to approach it in exactly that way, because I think that as dancers, you're so aiming to please, and you want to do exactly what the choreographer is asking of you. So I think you're right — it's so much better if the choreographer really gives that permission, because otherwise it is really hard for the dancers to speak up.

[37:56] **Adji Cissoko:** But I do also have to say that sometimes we have these aches and pains and we think, oh, maybe I should take it easy. But sometimes no — commit to it full out. When you are not really hurting, that is going to strengthen you. So it's really also our responsibility to learn more and more about what is really an injury moment and what is just fatigue or soreness — because we don't want to mistake that. We do want to push when the time is right, and then lay off when it's not. So really trying to learn more about your body at all times is just so important.

[38:39] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** Yeah, absolutely. Hurt versus harm, right? Something can hurt, but it's not actually harmful.

[38:45] **Adji Cissoko:** You want to use your willpower to push through it so that you realize, oh yeah, I'm okay.

[38:52] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** Boy, we could summarize this entire podcast in what you said at the very beginning: dancers listening to their body. Because that really is the key. [39:03] **Adji Cissoko:** And I think the last thing I will say about even just the whole choreography process — I don't usually like for the dancers to focus on the mirror when they are trying things out or coming up with their own movement, because then they're so focused on, is that height correct, is that foot right — and I'm more interested in what comes out of you so that we are actually creating something new. And usually when you listen more to your body, you won't do something that's actually strenuous or hurting yourself. It's more when we are so focused on the looks of it that we force things.

[39:49] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** Yeah. That's an interesting point. And there's some very fascinating literature in the dance medicine world about mirrors, especially in ballet. [40:04] **Adji Cissoko:** Yeah. I think usually I'm always like, let's face away from the mirror. But I think there's also a reason why we have mirrors, and that is especially for young dancers — sometimes we explain something verbally, but you also need to see it at some point to really understand it. So I think there's a balance between understanding it internally and sometimes needing to see something to get it. But in general, I think the mirrors are often overused and they're not actually helping us.

[40:37] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** You are so incredibly wise. [42:09] **Adji Cissoko:** I think it's just a reminder that we need to sometimes be our own pick-me-up. Teachers, choreographers, people you work with — they almost don't have the time to always tell you, that was good, that was wonderful, and this is what we need to improve. [42:46] And then celebrating individuality and reminding yourself that it's literally impossible to be like somebody else. But in that beauty, we still realize that these are all many different people. It's not possible to be exactly the same, but you want to all have the same idea and be on the same page, right? So that when we hear the music, we all want to hear it the same way so that we can execute that music and movement the same way as a corps de ballet.\
[43:36] But then what I find even more special is if you have a group of people and they are all reading the same book on the same page, maybe the same word, but yet they're interpreting it very individually. For me, that's even more beautiful to see. In classical ballet there is a moment where we all need to move exactly the same — but with that, you shouldn't forget that you're all different too, and that is beautiful. We can all train to do the same movement, but yet give it your individual feeling so that it doesn't feel forced or put on. You want to become the movement. [44:29] **Dr. Linda Bluestein:** Yeah, definitely. And I think that self-esteem coming from within is such an important point, because I think so often we are looking for external validation. And for a lot of us who struggle with that, there's never going to be enough — that's the other problem.

[44:48] **Adji Cissoko:** But also knowing that there is no perfection. Everybody — even the most perfect dancer that you think of, the one you're like, oh, they have it all together — they don't. [45:12] It's really about how, when those thoughts come in, can you switch them over and be like, yeah, I do feel a little bit insecure about this one — let me approach it and really figure it out. And with that, there's less of, I'm just off today. Because that's not going to help at all.
