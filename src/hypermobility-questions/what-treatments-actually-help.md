There is no single treatment that works for everyone with hEDS or HSD. The most effective care is individualized and focuses on improving stability, reducing pain, and supporting day-to-day function rather than increasing flexibility.

The foundation of treatment is usually **targeted physical therapy**, ideally with a clinician familiar with hypermobility. The focus should be on slow, progressive strengthening, joint stabilization, proprioception training, and motor control. Stretching is often limited because excessive range of motion can worsen instability in many people.

**Activity pacing** is another key strategy. This involves balancing activity and rest to avoid symptom flares, rather than pushing through pain or deconditioning cycles. Learning personal thresholds for exertion can help reduce crashes, pain spikes, and fatigue.

**Pain management** may include a combination of approaches such as heat or cold therapy, gentle movement, certain medications when appropriate, and nervous system–focused strategies for chronic pain sensitization. Treatment is most effective when it avoids over-reliance on passive or purely short-term interventions.

Some individuals benefit from **bracing, taping, or assistive devices** to improve joint alignment and reduce injury risk during daily activities or exercise. These supports should be used strategically, not as a substitute for building strength, unless otherwise directed by a clinician.

Because hEDS and HSD often affect multiple systems, treatment may also include management of related conditions such as dysautonomia, gastrointestinal symptoms, headaches, or sleep disturbance. Coordinated care across specialties can be especially helpful when symptoms are complex.

Importantly, treatment should be cautious with interventions that are overly aggressive, focused only on flexibility, or not adapted for joint fragility. Progress is usually gradual and measured in improved function, reduced flares, and increased stability rather than complete symptom elimination.
